Correlation vs regression


1. Overview

Take note:

  1. Correlation:
    ➊ Association between two quantitative variables.
    ➋ Linear association; in which one variable increases/decreases for each increase/decrease in the other.
    ➌ Quantifies strength of linear relationship between a pair of variables.
  2. Regression:
    ➊ Estimating the best straight line to summarise the association.
    ➋ Expresses the relationship in the form of an equation.
  3. Examples:
    🌟 Correlation: determine whether there is a relationship between age and urea level.
    🌟 Regression: determine whether the level of urea can be predicted for a given age.


2. Correlation coefficient

Take note:

  1. The degree of association is measured by a correlation coefficient, r.
  2. Pearson’s correlation coefficient: measure of linear association.
  3. Correlation coefficient is measured from +1 to –1.
    🌟 Complete/perfect correlation between two variables is expressed by either +1 or -1.
  4. Positive correlation: When A increases, B increases.
    (or decreases-decreases)
    Negative correlation: When A increases, B decreases.
    (or decreases-increases)
    No correlation: represented by 0.
  5. Scatter diagram:
    🌟 constructed to see whether there is a relationship between two variables.
    🌟 y-axis: dependent
    🌟 x-axis: independent

  6. 🌟 The scatter diagram for urea and age suggests there is a positive linear relationship between these variables.
    🌟 The closer the points lie to a straight line, the stronger the linear relationship between two variables. This can be quantified by correlation coefficient.
  7. Non-linear relationship is depicted like this:
  8. Correlation is not causation.
  9. Strength of association: r=
    0 - 0.19 ➠ very weak
    0.2 - 0.39 ➠ weak
    0.4 - 0.59 ➠ moderate
    0.6 - 0.79 ➠ strong
    0.8 - 1 ➠ very strong
  10. To test whether the association might have arisen by chance, use t test. The assumptions:
     ➊ both variables are normally distributed.
     ➋ linear relationship exists between them.
     ➌ null hypothesis: no association between them.

    ⚠️ Note that a significant result does not tell us about the strength of a relationship.

    ⚠️ A very weak relationship (eg. r = 0.1) would get a significant result with a large sample (eg. n = 1000).


3. Regression equation

Take note:

  1. Correlation describes the strength of an association between two variables, and is completely symmetrical;
    ➠ the correlation between A and B is the same as the correlation between B and A.
  2. If y = dependent variable, and x = independent variable, this relationship is described as the regression of y on x.

    ➠ Regression simply means that y changes with x.
  3. Regression equation is often more useful than the correlation coefficient.

    🌟 We can predict y from x.
  4. Example:
    x = height of children (cm)
    y = anatomical dead space of lung (ml)

    y = -82.4 + 1.033x
    ➠ On average, for every increase in height of 1 cm, the increase in anatomical dead space is 1.033 ml over the range of measurements made.
  5. Example:

    🌟 Regression line: urea = 0.72 + (0.017 × age).
    🌟 The gradient of this line is 0.017, which means for an increase of 1 year in age, the increase in urea is 0.017 units.
    🌟 In a patient aged 60 years,
    ➠ urea = 0.72 + (0.017 × 60)
    ➠ urea = 1.74 units
  6. Multiple regression:
    ➠ Predict dependent variable from multiple independent variables.
